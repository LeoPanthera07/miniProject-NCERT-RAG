1. The scenario
PariShiksha is a 4-year-old edtech startup operating across Tier-2 and Tier-3 cities in Rajasthan, Madhya
Pradesh, and Uttar Pradesh. They run after-school NCERT-aligned study centres for Class 9 and 10
students, with about 4,200 students currently enrolled across 18 centres. Parents pay a modest monthly
fee. Margins are thin. Tutor availability is the bottleneck on growth.
The founders want to pilot a bounded study assistant: something that answers NCERT Science questions
reliably when a tutor is not available. The non-negotiable is that the assistant must stay grounded in
NCERT content. A parent whose daughter was told something contradicting the textbook would escalate,
and the trust damage would kill the pilot.
You are the sole ML engineer on a three-person prototype team. A senior backend engineer handles
infrastructure. A Class 9 Science teacher is on contract to help build the evaluation set. You have this
week to build the retrieval-ready foundation — a system that extracts, structures, and queries one or two
chapters of NCERT Science content, generates grounded answers, and can be honestly evaluated on
whether it works.
You will not build a full vector-database RAG pipeline this week. That is next week's project. This week is
about making the content retrieval-ready, the generation grounded, and the evaluation real. If this
foundation is weak, next week's work will collapse onto it.
WHY THE SCENARIO MATTERS
Every week's project is built around a realistic context, not a generic exercise. The scenario shapes what
"correct" means for your system. PariShiksha serves students in cities where Hindi-English
code-switching is normal, where data connectivity is unreliable, and where tutor trust is the product's
moat. Keep this in mind when you design your evaluation and when you write your reflection.

2. What this project exercises
This week you have been studying tokenization, encoder-decoder architectures, transformer internals,
generative models (including GANs as a contrast), and the major transformer variants. The project is
designed so that each of those topics shows up as a practical decision you must make — not as a section
in a report.
Topic
Where it appears in the project
Tokenization
Your chunking strategy begins with a tokenizer choice. Different tokenizers split
scientific terms (photosynthesis, specific-heat-capacity) differently. Stage 1 asks
you to compare at least two.
Encoder-decoder models
Stretch asks you to try a T5-family (encoder-decoder) model alongside a
decoder-LLM. You will feel the difference in generation behavior on the same
retrieved context.
Attention and context
Chunks that are too large waste context window and dilute attention; chunks that
are too small lose surrounding reasoning. Your chunk-size experiment in Stretch
surfaces this directly.
Transformer variants
Three architectural families sit behind three choices: BERT-style extractive QA,
T5-style generative QA, and decoder-only LLMs (like Gemini). The project lets
you compare at least two of them on the same evaluation set.
GANs (this week's odd one
out)
GANs are not used in this project. Reflection prompt 3 asks you to explain why
GAN-style adversarial generation is the wrong tool for grounded textbook QA.
The answer teaches you something important about matching generative
architectures to problems.
Evaluation discipline
Stage 4 is where most students discover how little they know about their own
system. Your evaluation set is the measure of your engineering maturity this
week.
And what it sets up for next week
Next week is advanced RAG — dense embeddings, hybrid retrieval, cross-encoder reranking, guardrails,
and production concerns. All of that composes on top of this week's foundation. Students who build this
week carelessly will spend next week fighting bugs they introduced now. Students who build this week well
start next week with design judgment.

EXPERT HINT · Why this content is appropriately messy
You will not find this corpus pre-cleaned on Kaggle or Hugging Face. The messiness is the point, and it
comes from PDF extraction itself:
• Formula rendering is inconsistent — some equations come through as text, some as images, some as
mojibake
• Figure captions reference diagrams you will not have, creating dangling references
• Section boundaries are not cleanly marked in extracted text
• Concept paragraphs, worked examples, and end-of-chapter questions have different structures, but flat
extraction flattens them into one stream
A submission that says "the corpus was clean" is a submission that did not look at it carefully.
3.5 Collaboration policy
Collaborating with teammates on environment setup is fine and encouraged — it is not the part being
graded. Using AI tools to debug setup issues is fine. The experiments, the evaluation, and the reflection
are your own work.

4. Task ladder
You progress from Base outward. Base is required; Stretch, Advanced, and Open are extensions. Nothing
is gated — you attempt higher tiers when you are ready, not when someone unlocks them.
Base (required)
Five stages, executed in order. Each stage produces evidence in your notebook.
Stage 1 — Corpus
•
Extract text from one or two chapters using PyMuPDF or pdfplumber
•
Split the text into at least three content types where possible: concept paragraphs, worked examples,
end-of-chapter questions
•
Compare at least two tokenizers (GPT-2 BPE, BERT WordPiece, T5 SentencePiece) on five
representative passages — report token counts and where boundaries disagree
•
Design a chunking strategy (size, overlap, handling) and justify the choices in 150–250 words
Stage 2 — Retrieval
•
Build a chunk store where each chunk carries metadata: chapter name, section, content type
•
Implement a lexical retriever using BM25 (preferred) or TF-IDF, returning top-k chunks for a query
•
Test with three questions from your chapters and confirm the retrieved chunks are relevant
Stage 3 — Generation
•
Integrate an LLM (API recommended) for answer generation
•
Design a grounding prompt that instructs the LLM to answer only from provided context and to refuse
when the answer is not in context
•
Produce a callable function answer(question) -> {answer, retrieved_chunks}
Stage 4 — Evaluation
•
Build an evaluation set of 15–20 questions: 10–12 direct from textbook, 2–3 paraphrased, 3–5
out-of-scope
•
Score each question on three axes: correctness (yes/no/partial), grounding (is the answer supported by
retrieved chunks?), refusal-appropriateness (for out-of-scope only)
•
Save results as evaluation_results.md or .csv
•
Identify three working examples and two failing examples; for each failure, write one sentence on
probable cause
Stretch
•
Model family comparison. Run the same evaluation set through at least two model families: Extractive
QA (deepset/roberta-base-squad2), encoder-decoder (google/flan-t5-small or
flan-t5-base), decoder-LLM (the API you chose). Pick any two. Record differences in correctness
and grounding.
•
Attention inspection. Pick one question where flan-t5-small answered correctly and one where it got it
wrong. Visualize attention on the encoder's final layer. Write 100–150 words on what you saw.

5. Expert hints
These are the kinds of things a senior engineer would tell you across a table if you said "I'm building a
RAG system for the first time." None of them are in the standard tutorials. Each one matches a mistake
most people make on their first attempt. Read them once now and again after you have a working Base.
EXPERT HINT · Retrieval-time and index-time tokenization must match
If you tokenize your corpus with one tokenizer (BPE) but tokenize your queries with another
(WordPiece), your BM25 scores are meaningless. For BM25 this rarely bites because you probably use
a simple word-tokenizer both sides. But the moment you add a neural reranker or dense retriever next
week, this asymmetry will silently corrupt your scores. Make tokenizer choice an explicit decision, not an
accident of which library you called.
EXPERT HINT · Chunk boundaries that split an example from its solution destroy retrieval
If chunk 7 contains "Example: a car moving at 20 m/s..." and chunk 8 contains the worked solution, a
student asking "how do I solve the car example?" retrieves chunk 7 (the problem) but not chunk 8 (the
answer). The LLM then confidently generates a wrong solution. Before you set your chunk size, look at
your actual content and identify which semantic units must stay together. Overlap is your friend; pure
fixed-size chunking is a blunt tool.
EXPERT HINT · The most common hallucination is not "model invents facts"
It is "model dutifully generates from whatever context it was given, even if that context is irrelevant."
When your system hallucinates, your first instinct will be to blame the LLM. Nine times out of ten the bug
is upstream — your retriever returned a plausible-looking but wrong chunk, and the LLM did exactly what
you told it to. Always print the retrieved chunks for a failing query before you touch the prompt or change
the model.
EXPERT HINT · "Only answer from context" is weaker than "refuse if not in context"
The first instruction is permissive — the LLM can interpret it as "prefer the context." The second is a
constraint — the LLM must check whether the answer is actually present, and refuse if it isn't. Most
grounding failures come from the first phrasing. Test both explicitly. You will see a difference.
EXPERT HINT · Set temperature = 0 for evaluation, or your scores are not reproducible
If you score your system at 14/20 today and 11/20 tomorrow with the same eval set and no code
changes, the problem is decoding non-determinism. For evaluation use temperature=0 (or the API's
equivalent). For the user-facing experience you might want some temperature; those are two different
things. Never confuse them.
EXPERT HINT · The gap between good chunking and bad chunking is larger than the gap between LLMs
If your Base scores 12/20 with Gemini and GPT-4 would have scored 14/20 on the same questions, that
is a 10% improvement. If better chunking would have taken the same Gemini system to 17/20, that is a
25% improvement. This week, spend your time on your chunks and your prompt. Do not chase LLM
quality before fixing the upstream.

EXPERT HINT · flan-t5-small will hallucinate on reasoning questions because of its size
flan-t5-small has 77 million parameters. For comparison, Gemini 1.5 Flash has on the order of billions. If
you use flan-t5-small on a question that requires multi-step reasoning over the context ("which of these
forces is greater and why?"), it will often produce fluent but wrong answers. This is not a prompt problem;
it is a capacity problem. Use flan-t5-small for comparison, for structural analysis, for showing your
variant-comparison — not as the production choice for this use case.
EXPERT HINT · When iterating on your grounding prompt, change one variable at a time
The strongest temptation when evaluation is going badly is to rewrite the prompt, change the chunk size,
and swap the model all in the same commit. Then the next eval run is better and you do not know which
change helped. Engineers who have shipped real systems know that single-variable iteration is slower in
the short term and faster in the medium term. Commit after each change. Your git log becomes your
experiment log.
EXPERT HINT · Out-of-scope questions that retrieve relevant-looking chunks are the real adversarial test
The easy out-of-scope test is "Who is the Prime Minister of India?" — your retriever returns nothing
relevant, the LLM refuses, you claim victory. The real test is "Explain quantum entanglement in Chapter
9 of NCERT" — your retriever returns Chapter 9 content that is not about quantum entanglement, and a
weak grounding prompt will happily synthesize a confident wrong answer from unrelated context. Include
at least one of these in your eval set. This is the question that separates systems that pilot from systems
that don't.
EXPERT HINT · Real student questions are messier than the ones you will write yourself
If you build your own eval set, you will unconsciously phrase questions the way a textbook would. Real
students type questions with grammar errors, missing punctuation, Hindi-English code-switching, and
half-remembered terms ("what is newton 2nd law force equal mass time acceleration"). If time permits,
ask someone outside the cohort to write five questions for your eval set without looking at the textbook.
The quality drop will teach you something.

6. What "done" looks like
Base complete
A runnable notebook that extracts one chapter, compares two tokenizers, builds a BM25-retrieved chunk
store, generates grounded answers via an LLM API, and produces a real evaluation table on 15–20
questions with at least 10 correct, 3+ refusals handled appropriately, and 2 honest failure cases analyzed.
Stretch complete
Base plus a comparison across two model families with measurable score differences, plus one attention
inspection, plus one chunking experiment — with a written conclusion about which configuration you would
take forward.
Advanced complete
Stretch plus a dense-retrieval comparison, explicit guardrails, and a failure-mode document that reads as if
written for a real product team.
NOTE ON EFFORT VS. TIER
A thoughtful Base submission will often score higher than a rushed Advanced submission. The rubric
weights evaluation quality and grounding because those are what decide whether this pilot survives. Do
the work in order; do not skip to the glossy parts.

9. Interview-flavor scenarios
These are the kinds of questions you will face in interviews for GenAI Engineer, LLM Application Engineer,
and AI Platform roles. They are case-study style — there is no single correct answer, and the interviewer
is evaluating how you think, not what you memorized.
You do not need to submit answers to these. Practice them — with a teammate, in front of a mirror, out
loud on a walk. The goal is to get comfortable talking through ambiguous technical decisions.
EXPERT HINT · How to approach case-study questions without anxiety
Case-study interviewers are not trying to catch you out. They want to see how you reason under
uncertainty. A junior engineer who says "I would start by asking what the current failure modes are, then
pick the fix that addresses the most common one" shows better judgment than one who confidently picks
an answer without acknowledging the tradeoffs.
Three things that land well: (1) name the thing you would need to know before deciding, (2) walk through
your reasoning aloud even when uncertain, (3) acknowledge what you don't know rather than bluff.
Interviewers reward honest thinking over confident guessing. If you have actually built something this
week, you have real experiences to draw from — use them.
INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The domain-shift question
"A pharmaceutical company wants a similar RAG system reading FDA regulatory filings. What are three
changes you would make compared to your PariShiksha build? Which of those changes is the most
important, and why?"
What the interviewer is looking for: can you reason from context to architecture? Good answers identify
that pharma means higher liability, cross-document reasoning, exact citation requirements, and possibly
multi-lingual (English + EU languages). Great answers name which single change matters most and
defend the ranking.
INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The scale question
"Your PariShiksha system works at prototype scale. Walk me through what breaks first as we move from
18 centres to 500 centres and from 15 eval questions to 50,000 queries per day."
What the interviewer is looking for: production thinking. Answers should touch on latency under
concurrent load, cost per query at scale (LLM API bills become real), evaluation drift as real queries
diverge from your hand-picked set, and monitoring — how would you even know if the system started
hallucinating more next month?
INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The tradeoff question
"You have 10 additional hours of engineering time. You must choose exactly one: (a) better chunking, (b)
better grounding prompt, (c) dense retrieval, (d) explicit guardrails. Which, and why?"
What the interviewer is looking for: decision-making under constraints. The "correct" answer depends on
what your system's current failure modes actually are. The strongest answers say "it depends on what
my evaluation shows is currently weakest — if most failures are hallucinations, I pick (b); if most failures
are retrieval misses, I pick (a) or (c); if most failures are out-of-scope issues, I pick (d)." Weak answers
pick a tier without justification.

INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The failure-mode question
"Tell me about a time a system you built failed in a surprising way. Walk me through how you figured out
what was wrong."
What the interviewer is looking for: engineering maturity. This is the question where your reflection
questionnaire answers become gold. Students who genuinely debugged their system have a story;
students who used an LLM to answer the questionnaire do not. The honest specific story wins every time
over the impressive-sounding generic one.
INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The architecture whiteboard question
"Draw the architecture of your PariShiksha system on the whiteboard. Now, if we needed to handle
questions in both English and Hindi (because students code-switch), what changes? Draw those
changes."
What the interviewer is looking for: can you extend an existing design to a new requirement? Good
answers identify that tokenizer behavior on Devanagari differs from English, that embedding models
trained primarily on English may degrade, and that evaluation sets must include Hindi queries. Great
answers also note that pretrained models vary in multilingual quality and name specific ones (e.g.,
multilingual MPNet, XLM-R) that would help.
INTERVIEW FLAVOR · The "what if it breaks in production" question
"Your assistant has been live for two weeks. A parent calls, furious, saying the assistant told her
daughter that Newton's second law is F = mv instead of F = ma. How do you investigate?"
What the interviewer is looking for: ops thinking and debugging discipline. You should be able to sketch a
debugging playbook: reproduce the specific query, inspect what chunks were retrieved, check the
generation trace, determine whether this is a retrieval miss or a generation error, and propose both
immediate mitigation (e.g., add this case to eval set and block deployment until it passes) and root cause
fix.

finds a hallucination.
A student who builds a working pipeline and then tests it seriously — including questions it should refuse
— will learn more than a student who builds something fancier and tests it on three easy questions.
Take the evaluation seriously. Write the grounding prompt carefully. Let your system fail on something and
trace the cause. That is the work.
